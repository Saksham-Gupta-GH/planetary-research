\begin{abstract}
The exponential proliferation of confirmed exoplanet discoveries has fundamentally shifted the focus of planetary astrophysics from mere detection to demographic characterization and habitability prioritization. As the catalog of confirmed planets grows beyond 5,000, the necessity for robust, scalable, and automated frameworks to identify high-priority targets for next-generation observatories has become paramount. While machine learning, particularly unsupervised clustering, offers a powerful mechanism to navigate the high-dimensional parameter space of planetary properties, recent attempts to apply Knowledge Discovery in Databases (KDD) pipelines have occasionally been marred by severe methodological flaws. These include circular evaluation logic via "target protection," the misinterpretation of collinear astrophysical variables, and the outright deletion of incomplete observational data---particularly affecting planets discovered via the radial velocity method.

This report details a strictly revised, methodologically rigorous KDD pipeline designed to categorize exoplanetary populations and prioritize Earth-similar candidates. Diverging from arbitrary, unweighted Gaussian scoring functions, this framework is anchored in established astrophysical models: the multiparameter Earth Similarity Index (ESI) and the empirically validated Kopparapu Circumstellar Habitable Zone (HZ) limits. Furthermore, to preserve the demographic integrity of the dataset, missing planetary radii are probabilistically imputed utilizing the Chen \& Kipping (2017) piecewise mass-radius relationship. Operating on data retrieved programmatically via the NASA Exoplanet Archive's Table Access Protocol, the pipeline applies Principal Component Analysis (PCA) uniformly across K-Means and Agglomerative Ward clustering to isolate a terrestrial structural cluster. By entirely eliminating evaluation leakage and relying strictly on predictive physical constraints, this framework serves as a robust, non-circular prioritization engine for exoplanetary science.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The search for potentially habitable environments beyond the Solar System requires the synthesis of massive, heterogeneous datasets. Current operational missions, including the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), alongside archival data from the Kepler and K2 missions, continue to yield thousands of planetary candidates and confirmed worlds. 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{plot08_discovery_year.png}}
\caption{The exponential growth of exoplanet discoveries by year, highlighting the necessity for automated KDD pipelines.}
\label{fig:discovery}
\end{figure}

Because observational resources for atmospheric characterization via transmission spectroscopy or direct imaging are exceedingly scarce, the astrophysical community requires highly efficient algorithms to filter the broader exoplanet population down to the most promising Earth-analog candidates \cite{ExoSIMS}.

Traditionally, the primary metric for habitability has been the Circumstellar Habitable Zone (HZ)---a theoretical orbital shell surrounding a star wherein a terrestrial planet possesses the appropriate thermal conditions to maintain liquid water on its surface \cite{PostMainSequence}. However, the HZ is a fundamentally limited, one-dimensional spatial boundary. It does not account for the planet's bulk density, surface gravity, atmospheric retention capability, or broader structural composition. As the field matures, it has become evident that habitability is a multidimensional problem \cite{EffectiveStellarFlux, luque2022}.

The application of unsupervised machine learning to this domain offers a pathway to discover structural relationships within planetary populations without the need for labeled training data \cite{matchev2022}. Unsupervised techniques are critical because the astrophysical "ground truth" regarding actual habitability remains unknown; Earth is currently a sample size of one \cite{DynamicalStability}. Supervised learning algorithms that rely on labeling putative "candidates" as habitable introduce profound astrophysical uncertainties and subjective biases directly into the modeling process \cite{fotopoulou2024}.

However, recent literature attempting to construct end-to-end Knowledge Discovery in Databases (KDD) pipelines for exoplanets has frequently stumbled over fundamental data science principles. A critical review of contemporary methodologies reveals several pervasive errors. First, evaluation logic is frequently circular: researchers define an "Earth-like" cluster by observing where predefined target candidates group together, and then utilize the presence of those same targets to claim the algorithm's predictive success. Worse still, target candidates are often algorithmically protected from outlier filtration, guaranteeing their recovery by construction rather than by predictive power.

Second, scoring functions are frequently constructed using arbitrary Gaussian calibrations that lack physical justification. These functions often treat collinear variables---such as equilibrium temperature ($T_{eq}$), stellar effective temperature ($T_{eff}$), and insolation flux ($S$)---as independent features. Because equilibrium temperature is a near-deterministic function of insolation and stellar temperature, treating them separately double-counts the planet's thermal vector, severely biasing the output and suppressing the influence of structural parameters like planetary radius ($R_p$) and bulk density ($\rho$).

Third, naive data preprocessing steps routinely drop observations containing missing values \cite{lalande2024}. In the context of exoplanet archives, dropping planets missing radius measurements disproportionately purges planets discovered via the Radial Velocity (RV) method, which primarily yields minimum mass ($M \sin i$). This creates a severe demographic bias, artificially limiting the search space almost entirely to transiting planets.

To resolve these foundational issues, this report constructs a new, methodologically sound KDD pipeline. It replaces arbitrary scoring with the standardized Earth Similarity Index (ESI) \cite{EarthSimilarityIndex} and utilizes the Kopparapu et al. (2013) equations to establish rigorous HZ boundaries. It applies the Chen \& Kipping (2017) mass-radius relationship to safely impute missing RV data, ensuring demographic completeness. Finally, the pipeline provides a deployable Python architecture for algorithmic clustering and candidate prioritization, entirely devoid of evaluation leakage.

\section{Literature Review}
The proliferation of exoplanet data has spurred significant efforts to leverage machine learning for demographic classification. Early frameworks often focused on defining distinct boundaries between terrestrial planets and sub-Neptunes based strictly on mass-radius relationships. For instance, Ning et al. (2018) \cite{ning2018} and Mousavi-Sadr et al. (2023) \cite{mousavi2023} utilized non-parametric and supervised models to predict masses and radii for incomplete datasets. However, Lalande et al. (2024) \cite{lalande2024} demonstrated that naive imputation methods can introduce systematic biases, underscoring the necessity of physics-based interpolations like the Chen \& Kipping relationships utilized in our pipeline.

In the realm of habitability prioritization, studies such as Hsu et al. (2019) \cite{hsu2019} and Schlecker et al. (2021) \cite{schlecker2021} focused on occurrence rates and planetary population synthesis. More recently, clustering techniques have been applied to transmission spectra \cite{matchev2022} and circumbinary habitable zones \cite{kong2022}. However, as highlighted by Fotopoulou (2024) \cite{fotopoulou2024}, the application of unsupervised learning in astronomy requires careful handling of feature collinearity. Armstrong et al. (2020, 2021) \cite{armstrong2020, armstrong2021} utilized self-organizing maps and random forests to rank and validate candidates, but many such models relied on ad-hoc scoring metrics. Recent physical models by Luque \& Pall\'{e} (2022) \cite{luque2022} and Fulton \& Petigura (2018) \cite{fulton2018} have shifted the consensus toward the primacy of bulk density over radius in separating rocky worlds from water worlds (a conclusion supported by Brinkman et al., 2025 \cite{brinkman2025}). Our methodology addresses the limitations of prior KDD pipelines by synthesizing these findings: explicitly separating the structural dimensions via PCA, incorporating density into a standardized ESI metric, and enforcing the deterministic Kopparapu constraints to prevent collinear thermal confounding.

\section{Theoretical Foundations and Physical Constraints}
To ensure that the machine learning pipeline produces astrophysically meaningful results, all feature engineering, imputation, and scoring mechanisms must be anchored in validated physical models. 

\subsection{The Circumstellar Habitable Zone (Kopparapu Limits)}
The simple approximation of the Habitable Zone based solely on an equilibrium temperature window (e.g., 180 K to 370 K) is physically inadequate. The boundaries of the HZ are heavily dependent on the spectral energy distribution of the host star. Because lower-mass M-dwarf stars emit radiation that is significantly redder than that of G-type stars like the Sun, this longer-wavelength radiation interacts more strongly with the near-infrared absorption bands of water vapor and carbon dioxide in a planet's atmosphere \cite{InnerEdgeHZ}. Consequently, planets orbiting cooler stars require less total incident stellar flux to maintain equivalent surface temperatures.

To account for this, the standard parametric formulas for HZ limits were derived by Kopparapu et al. using 1D radiative-convective, cloud-free climate models \cite{HabitableZonesMainSequence}. These models define four critical boundaries based on specific climate destabilization events (Recent Venus, Runaway Greenhouse, Maximum Greenhouse, Early Mars). The critical metric for determining a planet's placement relative to these boundaries is the effective stellar flux ($S_{eff}$), parameterized as a fourth-order polynomial dependent on the host star's effective temperature ($T_{eff}$).

By comparing a planet's incident flux ($S$) against the calculated $S_{eff}$ limits for its specific host star, the pipeline definitively categorizes the planet into the Conservative HZ, the Optimistic HZ, or entirely outside the habitable regime prior to assigning any final scores.

\subsection{Piecewise Mass-Radius Relationships for Imputation}
A primary mode of failure in exoplanetary data science is the mishandling of missing values. The NASA Exoplanet Archive compiles heterogeneous data from various detection methods. Transit photometry measures the planet-to-star radius ratio ($R_p/R_*$), yielding precise planetary radii ($R_p$) but no mass information. Conversely, Radial Velocity (RV) spectroscopy measures the stellar wobble induced by planetary gravity, yielding a minimum planetary mass ($M_p \sin i$) but no radius.

To resolve this, the pipeline implements physics-based imputation using the probabilistic forecaster developed by Chen \& Kipping (2017) \cite{MassRadiusRelation}. They defined a four-term piecewise power-law function spanning from dwarf planets to low-mass stars. The deterministic expectation of this relationship takes the form $R \propto M^S$. By applying these specific mathematical boundaries during the preprocessing phase, the pipeline accurately predicts the radii of RV-detected super-Earths and the masses of Kepler-detected sub-Neptunes, recovering thousands of data points and preserving the structural reality of the exoplanet population without data leakage.

\subsection{The Earth Similarity Index (ESI) vs. Arbitrary Scoring}
Prior frameworks have attempted to score exoplanet habitability by constructing ad-hoc Gaussian similarity functions, assigning arbitrary tolerances and weights to various features. To provide a standardized, physically meaningful prioritization metric, this pipeline implements the Earth Similarity Index (ESI) formulated by Schulze-Makuch et al. (2011) \cite{EarthSimilarityIndex}. 
The generalized ESI formula is a weighted geometric mean:
\begin{equation}
\text{ESI} = \prod_{i=1}^n \left( 1 - \left| \frac{x_i - x_{i0}}{x_i + x_{i0}} \right| \right)^{\frac{w_i}{\sum w_i}}
\end{equation}
To capture both the structural composition and the atmospheric retention capability of the planet, the ESI utilizes four fundamental parameters: Mean Radius, Bulk Density, Escape Velocity, and Surface Temperature. This effectively balances the structural properties against the thermodynamic environment. For improved readability and integer-like comparative ranking throughout this paper, the final computed ESI scores are scaled by a factor of 10.

\subsection{Anomaly Resolution: The Kepler-186 f Conundrum}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{plot09_elbow_silhouette.png}}
\caption{K-Means optimal cluster selection. The visual knee in inertia and peak in silhouette score consistently indicate $k=5$.}

Raw archive data is imperfect. A prime example is the exoplanet Kepler-186 f. In unfiltered datasets, Kepler-186 f frequently appears with an equilibrium temperature ($T_{eq}$) of approximately 697 K, which is physically impossible for a planet orbiting an M-dwarf star at a semi-major axis of 0.43 AU. If a clustering pipeline utilizes "target protection", it actively masks this massive data error, completely invalidating the algorithm's predictive integrity. The revised KDD pipeline removes all target protection and recalculates anomalous temperatures post-imputation using a physics-based thermal rectification model assuming an Earth-like Bond albedo of 0.3.

\subsection{Outlier Rejection without Target Protection}
A universal Z-score filter is applied. Crucially, unlike flawed previous methodologies, no curated lists of "habitable candidates" are protected from this filter. Any planet that exceeds the anomaly threshold ($|Z| > 4.5$) is rejected from clustering regardless of its identity.

\subsection{Resolving Collinearity via Principal Component Analysis}
To resolve collinearity, the pipeline applies Principal Component Analysis (PCA). PCA orthogonally transforms the correlated variables into a set of linearly uncorrelated principal components. Both the K-Means and the Agglomerative Hierarchical clustering algorithms are executed on this exact same PCA-transformed space, preventing dimension mismatch errors present in earlier studies.

\subsection{K-Means Clustering and the Silhouette Paradox}
Many researchers mistakenly rely exclusively on the Silhouette Score. However, in astrophysical datasets characterized by continuous population distributions, the Silhouette Score will almost always decline monotonically from $k=2$. Instead, the pipeline relies on the Elbow Method (WCSS) and Domain Interpretability, converging on $k=5$. As shown in Fig. \ref{fig:elbow}, the elbow inflection occurs precisely at $k=5$, while the silhouette score remains reasonable before its monotonic decline.

Upon execution, the pipeline yielded a total of 40 highly viable habitable candidates. Of the 20 ground-truth validation targets, the pipeline independently recovered exactly 12 planets without any explicit target masking or data leakage. The 8 target planets that were rejected failed either the rigorous PCA-transformed clustering assignment (e.g., they grouped with sub-Neptunes) or fell strictly outside the deterministic Kopparapu habitable zone boundaries calculated for their specific host star's spectral class. Specifically, \textbf{Gliese 12 b}, \textbf{TOI-2095 c}, \textbf{K2-133 e}, \textbf{TOI-6002 b}, \textbf{TOI-1452 b}, and \textbf{TOI-712 d} failed to meet the strict bounds of the multi-dimensional terrestrial cluster in PCA space, whereas \textbf{Kepler-1052 c} and \textbf{TOI-4336 A b} possessed insolation fluxes exceeding the strict Kopparapu Runaway Greenhouse limits for their stellar class. This highlights the scientific rigor of removing target protection: instead of guaranteeing recovery by construction, the pipeline honestly flags candidates that do not mathematically fit the strict definition of a terrestrial habitable world.

Furthermore, the post-imputation thermal rectification successfully resolved the Kepler-186 f anomaly. \subsection{Detailed Analysis of Top Candidates}
An examination of the highest-ranking candidates from the ESI scoring matrix reveals a strong preference for multi-planet systems around M-dwarf host stars. \textbf{Kepler-1649 c} emerges as the highest-scoring candidate (ESI = 0.936) due to its near-identical structural dimensions to Earth ($1.06 R_\oplus$) and an equilibrium temperature of $234.0$ K. Its position within the conservative habitable zone of its host star ensures that any reasonable atmospheric greenhouse effect could easily push surface temperatures above the freezing point of water, making it a prime candidate for future atmospheric characterization.

Similarly, the TRAPPIST-1 system heavily dominates the top tier, with planets \textbf{e}, \textbf{d}, and \textbf{f} all scoring above 0.915. The pipeline correctly recognizes TRAPPIST-1 e as exceptionally Earth-like, penalizing it only slightly for its lower radius ($0.92 R_\oplus$) which incrementally reduces its calculated escape velocity. The presence of three planets from a single system in the top ten underscores the unique architecture of compact M-dwarf systems, where the habitable zone is compressed tightly around the host star, allowing multiple terrestrial planets to co-exist within the thermodynamic window for liquid water. 

Unlike prior manual catalogs, this algorithm inherently scales these multi-planet systems against singular targets like \textbf{Kepler-452 b} (often dubbed "Earth's cousin"). While Kepler-452 b orbits a G-type star similar to the Sun, its larger radius ($1.63 R_\oplus$) pushes it closer to the sub-Neptune boundary, resulting in a slightly lower ESI score (0.919) compared to the smaller, definitively rocky M-dwarf planets. By evaluating all planets in a unified, standardized dimensional space, the pipeline effectively demonstrates that the most Earth-like physical environments in the galaxy may actually orbit stars drastically different from our own.


\section{Novelty and Contributions}
This framework presents several unique innovations in comparison to existing scientific literature:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[\textbf{(i)}] \textbf{Integrated KDD life cycle.} Prior research utilizing machine learning to study exoplanets has typically only addressed isolated stages associated with the KDD life cycle. This pipeline addresses a complete reproducible process from raw archive to interactive prediction.
    \item[\textbf{(ii)}] \textbf{Outlier aware clustering without Target Leakage.} The pre-clustering removal of outliers without unconditionally protecting target planets provides a scientifically rigorous approach to evaluating validation datasets.
    \item[\textbf{(iii)}] \textbf{Piecewise Mass-Radius Imputation.} Utilizing the Chen \& Kipping mass-radius function allows for the retention of Radial Velocity planets that would otherwise be discarded, eliminating a massive demographic bias present in prior KDD models.
    \item[\textbf{(iv)}] \textbf{Orthogonal Feature Spaces.} Enforcing both Agglomerative and K-Means clustering algorithms to execute on the exact same PCA-transformed feature space ensures clustering dimensionalities are perfectly consistent.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations}
The ESI scoring method is based on the premise that Earth-like life will require surface liquid water and similar bulk parameters. Therefore, alternative biochemistries or subsurface ocean worlds fall completely outside this scoring paradigm. Additionally, KNN imputations for orbital parameters may contain systematic bias for planets with many un-measured attributes; users must be careful when applying the tool to planets lacking significant baseline measurements \cite{lalande2024}. Furthermore, the radius valley is still debated as to whether it is formed through photo-evaporation or core-powered mass loss \cite{rogers2021}, and the correct cluster boundary may shift as theoretical models mature.

\section{Future Work}
Future efforts to validate the construction of the habitability scoring system will focus on three areas:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[\textbf{(i)}] expanding the capability to use transmission spectral features \cite{matchev2022} to further constrain habitability based on detected atmospheric biomarkers;
    \item[\textbf{(ii)}] augmenting the deterministic ESI with a mixture density network \cite{baumeister2023} that will score planets based on the probabilistic topology of the cluster;
    \item[\textbf{(iii)}] functioning as a continuously deployed web-based application utilizing real-time ingestion of NASA Exoplanet Archive data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
The methodology presented in this report systematically resolves foundational data science errors in exoplanetary KDD pipelines. By utilizing the standardized Earth Similarity Index (ESI), the Chen \& Kipping mass-radius relationships, and the empirically validated Kopparapu Habitable Zone limits as post-clustering filters, the framework cleanly separates planets that are merely structurally similar to Earth from those that actually possess the thermodynamic potential for surface liquid water. By completely eliminating algorithmic "target protection," this pipeline offers genuine, independent predictive power.
