\begin{abstract}
The vast increase in exoplanet discoveries has changed planetology from just being about detection to understanding what these planets are like and who might be habitable places. With over 5,000 confirmed exoplanets available, it has become necessary to have structured, efficient, and automated systems that assist in determining the exoplanets that need to be studied using new observational tools. Although machine learning and especially unsupervised clustering techniques present a valuable option for dealing with many variables that affect planets, several attempts to use knowledge discovery techniques have been limited by serious methodological shortcomings, including the circularity of the evaluation in the form of “target masking” and misinterpretation of collinear astronomical variables, as well as removal of incomplete observations, especially for those objects that used direct measurements.

This study proposes a thoroughly revised KDD pipeline that aims to classify exoplanetary classes and select the most suitable Earth-like exoplanets. Contrary to the application of simple ad hoc Gaussian scoring systems, the new method is based on reliable astrophysical theories, specifically, ESI and the well-known limitations of the Kopparapu HZ. Thus, in order to maintain the demographic characteristics of the dataset, missing planet radii values are imputed through a probabilistic approach using the mass-radius relationship of Chen \& Kipping (2017). The data is extracted programmatically through the Table Access Protocol of NASA Exoplanet Archive (NEA) and transformed using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) using both K-Means and Agglomerative Ward clustering to derive a terrestrial structural cluster. The framework enables eliminating any possibility of evaluation leakage and therefore can be described as a highly reliable and non-circular prioritization tool. 
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The search for an exoplanet involves the combination of numerous and various datasets. At present time, operations of the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), as well as archival data from Kepler and K2, continue generating thousands of planetary candidates and known planets. 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{plot08_discovery_year.png}}
\caption{The exponential growth of exoplanet discoveries by year, highlighting the necessity for automated KDD pipelines.}
\label{fig:discovery}
\end{figure}

One thing is certain that due to the limitation of observational means for atmospheric characterization through methods such as transmission spectroscopy or direct imaging, the science of exoplanets needs efficient algorithms for filtering out most promising candidates.\cite{ExoSIMS}.

Historically, the Circumstellar Habitable Zone (HZ) has represented the first principle for measuring habitability; this is a theoretical orbital shell surrounding a star where a terrestrial planet has been shown to be in thermal conditions allow for the existence of water in liquid state on its surface \cite{PostMainSequence}. Nonetheless, the HZ has been an inherently restricted one-dimensional phenomenon. There are several important factors ignored by HZ: the density of the planet, its surface gravity, its capability for retaining atmosphere and its greater structural component. Consequently, as the field has evolved, we have realized that the problem of habitability has a multi-dimensional character \cite{EffectiveStellarFlux}, \cite{luque2022}.

It is an excellent opportunity offered by employing unsupervised machine learning in order to unveil structural relations within planetary populations without employing any labeled training data \cite{matchev2022}. The significance of unsupervised methods is due to the fact that ground-truth astrophysical data on the issue of real habitability is impossible to obtain anywhere; at the moment Earth is the only example of habitability that most researchers utilize \cite{DynamicalStability}. Supervised learning models based on labeling habitable candidates contribute to astrological uncertainty and subjectivity in modeling \cite{fotopoulou2024}.


Despite of it, recent studies that aimed at putting together end-to-end Knowledge Discovery in Databases (KDD) processes for exoplanets have frequently fallen short due to methodological flaws. The detailed analysis of recent studies brings to light many mistakes made in this work. First of all, the reason why the evaluation method is circular is that in this research, an “earth-like” cluster is established through the analysis of data from previously discovered candidates, and then, after the definition of it, the data is used once again for the confirmation of earlier success of the methods applied. What is even worse about this is that the truth is often that the candidates are protected by methods and not by some kind of forecasting ability.

Second, scoring functions are frequently constructed using arbitrary Gaussian calibrations that lack physical justification. These functions often treat collinear variables---such as equilibrium temperature ($T_{eq}$), stellar effective temperature ($T_{eff}$), and insolation flux ($S$)---as independent features. Because equilibrium temperature is a near-deterministic function of insolation and stellar temperature, treating them separately double-counts the planet's thermal vector, severely biasing the output and suppressing the influence of structural parameters like planetary radius ($R_p$) and bulk density ($\rho$).

Finally, there is an ordinary data processing that eliminates the data with missing values all the time. As far as exoplanet archives are concerned, this makes the number of exoplanets identified by the RV method visible.

This report offers a newly built methodologically accurate KDD pipeline that fixes these principal problems. In the pipeline, arbitrary scoring has been replaced with the standardized Earth Similarity Index (ESI) \cite{EarthSimilarityIndex}, and Kopparapu et al. (2013) numbers have been used to set up the credible boundaries of HZ. The mass-radius relationship described by Chen \& Kipping (2017) has been used to safely fill in the holes in missing RV data. The pipeline uses a ready-made Python architecture for algorithmic clustering as well as for suggesting candidates, the technology does not produce any evaluation leakage.

\section{Literature Review}

\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth,height=0.85\textheight,keepaspectratio]{plot00_flowchart.png}}
\caption{System architecture and logic flow for the KDD pipeline. The algorithm progresses from raw data ingestion through unsupervised clustering, ultimately applying post-hoc physical constraints to identify habitable candidates without target leakage.}

The increasing amount of exoplanet data has made it possible to use different techniques of machine learning in classification studies. In the course of initial investigations, scientists tended to develop various boundaries between terrestrial and sub-Neptunian planets mainly based on their masses and radii. For example, authors like Ning et al. (2018) and Mousavi-Sadr et al. (2023) used supervised and nonparametric methods to find a mass and radius of planets with missing data. On the contrary, Lalande et al. (2024) proved that very simplistic imputation techniques could lead to systematic errors and emphasized the importance of using physics-based interpolation methods, for example, those proposed by Chen \& Kipping and applied in the project pipeline.

When it comes to the area of habitability prioritization, works such as those done by Hsu et al. (2019) \cite{hsu2019} and Schlecker et al. (2021) \cite{schlecker2021} have been focused on planetary occurrence rates and population synthesis respectively. In recent years, clustering techniques have also been used to analyze transmission spectra \cite{matchev2022} and circumbinary habitable zones \cite{kong2022}. Nevertheless, as was pointed out by Fotopoulou (2024) \cite{fotopoulou2024}, applying unsupervised learning in astronomy calls for careful consideration of feature collinearity. Armstrong et al. (2020, 2021) \cite{armstrong2021}, \cite{armstrong2020} employed random forests and self-organizing maps to rank and assess candidates, although many of those models depended on improvised scoring metrics. In recent works by Luque \& Pallé (2022) \cite{luque2022} and Fulton \& Petigura (2018) \cite{fulton2018}, a shift towards giving bulk density priority over radius in distinguishing rocky planets from water-rich planets has taken place (this idea was also backed up by Brinkman et al., in 2025 \cite{brinkman2025}). Our methodology addresses the limitations of prior KDD pipelines by synthesizing these findings: explicitly separating the structural dimensions via PCA, incorporating density into a standardized ESI metric, and enforcing the deterministic Kopparapu constraints to prevent collinear thermal confounding.

\section{Theoretical Foundations and Physical Constraints}
To ensure that the machine learning pipeline produces astrophysically meaningful results, all feature engineering, imputation, and scoring mechanisms must be anchored in validated physical models. 

\subsection{The Circumstellar Habitable Zone (Kopparapu Limits)}
The straightforward approximation of the Habitable Zone relying solely on an equilibrium temperature range (e.g., 180 K to 370 K) is not sound from a physical perspective. The limits of the HZ depend on the nature of the energy spectrum emitted by a star. Since less massive M-dwarf stars emit much redder radiation than G-type stars such as the Sun, their long-wave radiation will penetrate the near-infrared absorption bands of water vapor and carbon dioxide in the atmosphere of a planet more effectively \cite{InnerEdgeHZ}. As a result, planets revolving around cooler stars need a smaller value of the total incoming solar radiation in order to achieve similar temperatures on their surface.

To account for this, the standard parametric formulas for HZ limits were derived by Kopparapu et al. using 1D radiative-convective, cloud-free climate models \cite{HabitableZonesMainSequence}. These models define four critical boundaries based on specific climate destabilization events (Recent Venus, Runaway Greenhouse, Maximum Greenhouse, Early Mars). The critical metric for determining a planet's placement relative to these boundaries is the effective stellar flux ($S_{eff}$), parameterized as a fourth-order polynomial dependent on the host star's effective temperature ($T_{eff}$).

By comparing a planet's incident flux ($S$) against the calculated $S_{eff}$ limits for its specific host star, the pipeline definitively categorizes the planet into the Conservative HZ, the Optimistic HZ, or entirely outside the habitable regime prior to assigning any final scores.

\subsection{Piecewise Mass-Radius Relationships for Imputation}
A primary mode of failure in exoplanetary data science is the mishandling of missing values. The NASA Exoplanet Archive compiles heterogeneous data from various detection methods. Transit photometry measures the planet-to-star radius ratio ($R_p/R_*$), yielding precise planetary radii ($R_p$) but no mass information. Conversely, Radial Velocity (RV) spectroscopy measures the stellar wobble induced by planetary gravity, yielding a minimum planetary mass ($M_p \sin i$) but no radius..

To tackle this issue, the pipeline utilizes a physics-based imputation method involving the probabilistic forecaster created by Chen \& Kipping (2017) \cite{MassRadiusRelation}. Their approach includes a four-terms piecewise power-law function, which involves everything from dwarf planets to low mass stars. This relationship is expressed as a deterministic expectation $R \propto M^S$. By using this specific form of the mathematical boundaries in the preprocessing process, the pipeline is able to determine the radii of super-Earths detected by RV and the masses of sub-Neptunes discovered by Kepler successfully, thus retrieving a large quantity of data points and maintaining the actual structure of the population of exoplanets without leaking any data.

\subsection{The Earth Similarity Index (ESI) vs. Arbitrary Scoring}
Previous systems analyzed exoplanet habitability through dubious Gaussian functions with arbitrary parameters and weights. To create an objective and meaningful metric, we applied the formula of the Earth Similarity Index (ESI) by Schulze-Makuch et al. (2011) \cite{EarthSimilarityIndex}. 
\begin{equation}
\text{ESI} = \prod_{i=1}^n \left( 1 - \left| \frac{x_i - x_{i0}}{x_i + x_{i0}} \right| \right)^{\frac{w_i}{\sum w_i}}
\end{equation}
The generalized ESI formula includes four important scientific parameters—Mean Radius, Bulk Density, Escape Velocity, and Surface Temperature—which consider both the structure and the atmosphere of a given planet.

\subsection{Anomaly Resolution: The Kepler-186 f Conundrum}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{plot09_elbow_silhouette.png}}
\caption{K-Means optimal cluster selection. The visual knee in inertia and peak in silhouette score consistently indicate $k=5$.}
\label{fig:elbow}
\end{figure}

Raw archive data is imperfect. A prime example is the exoplanet Kepler-186 f. In unfiltered datasets, Kepler-186 f frequently appears with an equilibrium temperature ($T_{eq}$) of approximately 697 K, which is physically impossible for a planet orbiting an M-dwarf star at a semi-major axis of 0.43 AU. If a clustering pipeline utilizes "target protection", it actively masks this massive data error, completely invalidating the algorithm's predictive integrity. The revised KDD pipeline removes all target protection and recalculates anomalous temperatures post-imputation using a physics-based thermal rectification model assuming an Earth-like Bond albedo of 0.3.

\subsection{Exclusion of Outliers without Being Biased by Pre-determined Groups}
The universal Z-score tool is being utilized. In contrast to earlier methods, the algorithm does not take into account existing lists of "potentially habitable members". The anomaly value ($|Z| > 4.5$) is the only criterion used to reject the star.
\subsection{Dealing with Multicollinearity by Using Principal Component Analysis}
To deal with multicollinearity issues, the method relies on PCA. The latter transforms correlated variables into uncorrelated principal components. Then, both K-means and Hierarchical clustering are applied to this PCA space, avoiding drawbacks and limitations of previous research.

\subsection{K-means and the Paradox of Silhouette Score}
Many scientists rely only on the Silhouette Score. In astrophysical data that have continuous distributions of the population, the Silhouette Score tends to decrease monotonically from $k=2$. The method relies instead on the Elbow method (WCSS) and Domain Interpretability, arriving at $k=5$. Note that the elbow naturally occurs at $k=5$ according to Fig. \ref{fig:elbow}, but the Silhouette Score is not low – it decreases monotonically only after that point.

In order to validate the effectiveness of the mechanism, the author mentions the capability of receiving candidates suitable for habitability testing without any deliberate input targeting made by the system beforehand. As a result, the mechanism obtained 40 viable candidates in total. Among the 20 ground-truth validation targets, twelve have been retrieved independently. Eight planets that were singled out for rejection were found to have failed either the accurate PCA-based clustering assignment or did not qualify for the criteria set by determined Kopparapu habitable zone limits based on the properties of their respective host stars. Specifically, \textbf{Gliese 12 b}, \textbf{TOI-2095 c}, \textbf{K2-133 e}, \textbf{TOI-6002 b}, \textbf{TOI-1452 b}, and \textbf{TOI-712 d} failed to lie within the confines of the multi-dimensional terrestrial cluster in PCA space. Furthermore, \textbf{Kepler-1052 c} and \textbf{TOI-4336 A b} were found to exceed the Kopparapu Runaway Greenhouse limitations for their stellar class. Thus is established the scientific rigor of eliminating target protection: instead of simply guaranteeing the return of the objects by design, the algorithm rightly identifies the candidates not compliant with the strict mathematical definition of the terrestrial habitable world.
Moreover, the process of post-imputation thermal correction proved successful in removing the anomaly represented by Kepler-186 f. The algorithm successfully flagged the $T_{eq} = 697.4$ K value in the database as a huge anomaly given the insolation reaching $S = 0.30 S_\oplus$, and successfully remapped the temperature to a physically correct value of $205.7$ K, thereby providing an exceptionally high ESI score of 0.890. \subsection{Detailed Analysis of Top Candidates}
An examination of the top-ranking candidates based on the ESI scoring matrix suggests an unquestionable preference for multi-planet systems around M-dwarf stars. The case of \textbf{Kepler-1649 c} showcases itself as the highest-ranked candidate (ESI = 0.936) because of its similar physical characteristics relative to Earth ($1.06 R_\oplus$) and an equilibrium temperature of $234.0$ K. Located in the safe habitable zone around its host star, it is guaranteed that an adequate greenhouse effect can ensure temperatures above the freezing level of water and thus make it an excellent target for future atmospheric assessment. In the same situation, the TRAPPIST-1 system dominates the ranks with planets \textbf{e}, \textbf{d}, and \textbf{f} belonging to the highest-tier candidates, receiving scores above 0.915. The system successfully considered TRAPPIST-1 e highly Earth-like, punishing it only a little bit due to its smaller radius ($0.92 R_\oplus$). The existence of three planets having originated from one system in the top ten points out the special structure of compact M-dwarf systems, in which the habitable zone has a very narrow relationship with the host star and has several planets. While previous efforts have produced manually compiled catalogs of multi-planet systems, the algorithm presented here is inherently based on the scaling of multi-planet systems rather than on the scaling relative to individual targets, like \textbf{Kepler-452 b} (sometimes called "Earth's cousin"). Kepler-452 b revolves around a G-type star which is similar to the Sun, though the planet is much bigger ($1.63 R_\oplus$), close to the sub-Neptune cutoff. Thus, the score of this planet is a bit lower (0.919) compared to smaller M-dwarf planets. Thus, the approach helps to prove that the most Earth-like environments in the universe can belong to other stars.


\section{Novelty and Contributions}
The presented framework makes a number of innovative additions to the existing research in the field and can be summed up as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[\textbf{(i)}] \textbf{Integrated KDD Life Cycle.} Previous works in machine learning used for the study of exoplanets have focused on certain steps of the KDD life cycle only. The proposed pipeline solves the whole self-contained process from the raw archive to final prediction.
    \item[\textbf{(ii)}] \textbf{Outlier-aware Clustering without Target Leakage.} The outlier-removal step without the non-conditional safeguarding of the target planets implies a scientifically sound data-validation step.
    \item[\textbf{(iii)}] \textbf{Piecewise Mass-Radius Imputation.} The use of the direct relationship between the mass and radius of the exoplanets, as defined by Chen \& Kipping, allows for keeping the Radial Velocity discoveries that would have been eliminated; as a result, substantial demographic bias that was prevalent in earlier KDD models is reduced.
    \item[\textbf{(iv)}] \textbf{Orthogonal Feature Spaces.} The use of the combination of both hierarchical and K-Means clustering techniques implies that clustering is carried out on the same PCA-transformed feature space, which guarantees the conformity of the cluster dimensions.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations}
The ESI scoring methodology is based on the concept that an Earth-like form of life will need liquid water and associated bulk properties. Thus, alternative biochemistries or subsurface oceanic planets are completely outside of this scoring approach. Moreover, the KNN imputations for orbital parameters might have some systematic bias on planets that have many non-measured properties, so caution is needed when applying the instrument to planets without essential preliminary measurements \cite{lalande2024}. Furthermore, the radius valley formation debate remains unresolved, as photo-evaporation or core-powered mass loss remain candidate processes for its formation \cite{rogers2021}, and the standard boundary may move.

\section{Future Work}
Future efforts to validate the construction of the habitability scoring system will focus on three areas:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[\textbf{(i)}] expanding the capability to use transmission spectral features \cite{matchev2022} to further constrain habitability based on detected atmospheric biomarkers;
    \item[\textbf{(ii)}] augmenting the deterministic ESI with a mixture density network \cite{baumeister2023} that will score planets based on the probabilistic topology of the cluster;
    \item[\textbf{(iii)}] functioning as a continuously deployed web-based application utilizing real-time ingestion of NASA Exoplanet Archive data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
The framework outlined in this paper solves critical errors in exoplanet KDD pipelines in an organized manner. By applying the pre-determined measure of Earth Similarity Index (ESI), mass-radius relations created by Chen and Kipping, and confirmed Kopparapu Habitable Zone parameters accordingly after clustering is applied, we have been able to distinguish planets only but structurally similar to Earth from those which also have the right conditions allowing to preserve water in liquid state on their surface. Due to the absence of target protection algorithms, the proposed methodology is able to produce true and fair predictions.
